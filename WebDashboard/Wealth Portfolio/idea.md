# Executive Summary  This report outlines a comprehensive **web-based portfolio analytics dashboard** tailored for financial advisors. It will consolidate multiple clients’ portfolios into a unified interface, apply advanced risk models, and generate automated recommendations for rebalancing assets. Key functionalities include interactive, customizable dashboard widgets (drag‐and‐drop, resizable tiles), client/account grouping for aggregated views, and clear labeling of any test (“dummy”) data. The system will ingest transactions, holdings, market data, and client profiles via an ETL pipeline powered by [Polars](https://pola.rs/), enabling sub-second analytics. Risk will be assessed by multiple models – from classic mean–variance optimization and CAPM/factor models to tail-focused measures (VaR/CVaR), Monte Carlo simulation, and stress testing of extreme scenarios. The dashboard will feature one-click report generation (PDF/Excel) and interactive charts (e.g. drill-down allocation pie charts, time-series graphs). Underlying all features are strict data governance, encryption (TLS/AES-256), role-based access controls, and compliance with financial regulations【11†L278-L283】【35†L71-L79】.  
This solution will be built in phases, starting with core data ingestion and basic analytics, then adding advanced risk modules and UI polish, and finally optimizing for scale (e.g. distributed Polars) and performance. Roadmap milestones (3–6 month increments) ensure modular delivery and testing. The outcome will be a production-ready advisor dashboard supporting consolidated multi-account views, rapid risk analysis, and automated asset allocation guidance, backed by rigorous engineering and high-grade security.

Permissions (which user roles can view/manage this account).  - **Securities/Assets**: For each asset (ticker/ID) – name, asset class (equity, bond, cash, alternative), sector, region, risk attributes (beta, factor exposures), and possibly static attributes like ISIN.  - **Holdings/Lots**: By account, date: Ticker, quantity, cost basis, acquisition date, lotID. (Possibly fetched from vendors via API.)  - **Benchmarks and Risk Factor Series**: E.g. S&P 500 returns, Fama-French factors, interest rate curves, for use in factor models.

## Risk Models & Analytics  The dashboard will implement multiple risk/return models to give a full picture of portfolio health:
- **Mean–Variance Optimization (MVO)**: The classic Markowitz framework selects portfolios on the *efficient frontier* (maximizing expected return for a given volatility)【51†L444-L453】. Our system can run MVO to suggest an “optimal” asset mix given client targets. Inputs: historical return estimates, covariance matrix of asset returns. Advantages: well-understood, yields diversification trade-offs. Drawbacks: sensitive to input assumptions (unstable covariances), so regularization or shrinkage may be needed.  
- **Single-Factor (CAPM)**: Use the Capital Asset Pricing Model to estimate expected return vs. market risk. Calculate portfolio beta and expected return as E[R]=Rf + β*(E[Rm]–Rf). Useful for benchmarking portfolio performance vs. market. (A factor-1 simplification.)
- **Multi-Factor Models**: As detailed by the CFA Institute, multi-factor models express each asset’s return in terms of several risk factors【55†L252-L261】. For example, a Fama-French style model (market, size, value), or macro factor model (inflation surprise, GDP shock). These allow *risk attribution* (how much of volatility comes from each factor) and more nuanced expected return forecasts. The dashboard can include pre-built factor exposures (if data available) or let advisors define custom factors. Compared to CAPM, multi-factor models give a “granular” risk view【55†L252-L261】.
- **Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Conditional VaR (CVaR)**: Tail-risk measures. VaR at X% confidence estimates the worst loss not exceeded X% of the time. CVaR (Expected Shortfall) goes further by averaging losses beyond the VaR cutoff, capturing tail severity. CVaR is “the expected loss if that worst-case threshold is crossed”【48†L312-L319】. We will implement (parametric) variance-cov VaR and CVaR, as well as historical and Monte-Carlo variants. CVaR provides a conservative view of risk (focus on extreme losses)【48†L288-L294】.
- **Monte Carlo Simulation**: Randomly simulate thousands of portfolio return scenarios (e.g. drawing returns from a multivariate distribution or bootstrapping past returns) to generate a distribution of outcomes【45†L268-L276】. This yields forward-looking risk metrics: probability of loss >X%, distribution of terminal portfolio value, etc. Monte Carlo handles non-normal risks (e.g. option payoffs) and can incorporate custom shock distributions【45†L268-L276】. It is computationally intensive, but can be run offline nightly or on demand, potentially accelerated on cloud/GPU if needed. 
- **Stress Testing / Scenario Analysis**: Apply specific hypothetical shocks (e.g. 30% stock market crash, 2008-like credit freeze, rising rates) to the current portfolio to estimate losses. These “what-if” scenarios complement statistical models. For example, apply a sudden interest rate spike and reprice bonds, or simulate a recession to stress test equity portfolios. Regulatory-style stress tests have banks evaluate resilience under severe recessions【47†L457-L461】; similarly, advisors can use scenario templates or custom scenarios. Stress tests reveal vulnerabilities not seen in normal VaR metrics (e.g. liquidity squeezes, tail correlations).
**Model Comparison Table** (illustrative trade-offs):
| **Risk Model** | **Focus** | **Data Needed** | **Pros** | **Cons** ||---------------------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|| Mean–Variance (Markowitz) | Volatility & diversification | Expected returns, covariances | Widely understood; finds efficient frontier | Very sensitive to input estimates || CAPM | Market risk (β vs benchmark) | Market returns, portfolio beta | Simple benchmark view; single metric | Relies on broad market assumptions || Multi-Factor (e.g. APT) | Multiple systematic risks | Factor definitions & historical data | Granular attribution; accounts for varied drivers【55†L252-L261】 | Requires factor model selection || VaR (variance-cov) | Loss quantile | Covariance matrix, distribution | Fast; standard in risk management | Assumes normality; ignores tail beyond α || CVaR (ES) | Tail-end expected loss | As for VaR plus tail estimates | Captures severe losses beyond VaR【48†L288-L294】 | More complex; data-hungry tail estimates || Monte Carlo | Full outcome distribution | Full return model (or data to simulate) | Flexible; handles nonlinearities【45†L268-L276】 | Computationally heavy || Stress Testing | Extreme scenario impact | Scenario definitions (shocks) | Highlights hidden risks; regulatory precedent【47†L457-L461】 | Subject to scenario design bias |
For each portfolio, the system can compute multiple of these metrics. For example, show VaR95/CVaR95 side by side, or Sharpe/Sortino ratios (risk-adjusted returns). Advisors can toggle which models to display or use.
## Asset Allocation & Rebalancing  Once risk/return profiles are clear, the dashboard will recommend **rebalancing** rules to align portfolios with targets:
- **Target-Based Rebalancing**: Define target allocations (e.g. 60% stocks, 30% bonds, 10% alternatives) based on client risk. If actual weights drift beyond thresholds, suggest trades to rebalance. We can use either **threshold rebalancing** (if an asset class deviates ±X%) or **calendar rebalancing** (review quarterly/annually) or a hybrid (annual check plus drift trigger)【26†L143-L152】【26†L159-L168】.    - *Threshold example:* “Rebalance equity if >5% off target”.    - *Calendar example:* “Rebalance every 6 months” (possibly partial to reduce costs)【26†L175-L184】.
- **Account vs Household-Level**: For clients with multiple accounts (e.g. IRA + taxable), treat each as container or aggregate as one “household portfolio”. Household rebalancing tends to be more tax-efficient (shift gains into tax-sheltered accounts) and trade fewer shares【24†L449-L458】【24†L459-L467】. The system should let the advisor choose: per-account rebalancing (straightforward, no cross-account trades) or consolidated rebalancing across a household【24†L449-L458】【24†L459-L467】. The dashboard can show the difference in outcome.  
- **Tax-aware Rebalancing**: Incorporate tax considerations:   - For taxable accounts, prefer selling low-gain (high-cost-basis) lots or using dividends/cash flows to buy underweights【26†L189-L197】【26†L201-L205】.    - For IRAs, take advantage of tax shelter (e.g. put more growth assets there).    - Also use partial rebalancing to minimize trigger of capital gains【26†L189-L197】 (e.g. sell just enough to cross threshold).  
- **Automated Suggestions**: The dashboard can run a *rebalance optimizer*: given current weights and targets, compute the minimal trades (subject to tax rules) that bring the portfolio back into line. Then present an interactive proposal: e.g. “Sell $X of Fund A, Buy $Y of Fund B to reach targets.” Show projected impact on overall risk/return after trades. The advisor can approve or adjust (one-click apply to generate trade orders).  
A comparison of rebalancing approaches:  
| **Approach** | **Pros** | **Cons** ||-------------------------|--------------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|| Threshold-only | Simple; triggers only when needed【26†L143-L152】 | Requires monitoring; could trade frequently || Calendar-only | Easy schedule (e.g. annual review) | May trade unnecessarily if already close; timing might not match market cycles || Hybrid | Mix threshold + schedule【26†L159-L168】 | Slightly more complex logic || Account-level | Aligns with custodian constraints | May cause inefficient cross-account shifts【24†L449-L458】 || Household-level | More tax-efficient, fewer total trades【24†L459-L467】 | Complex (multiple accounts, rules, and custodians) |
By default, the system can suggest the **optimal rules** based on each client’s goals. For example, aggressive clients might tolerate wider band and less frequent rebalancing; retirees might rebalance conservatively to protect income.
